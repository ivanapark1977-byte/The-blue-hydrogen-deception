The Blue Hydrogen Deception

How management consulting firms are selling Gulf economies a climate dead end — and billing handsomely for the privilege.

Five core findings

1

Blue hydrogen is nearly as damaging as grey. Under realistic operating conditions — accounting for methane leakage of 1.5–3.5% and carbon capture rates of 85–90% rather than the theoretical 95% — blue hydrogen produces approximately 12 kg CO2eq per kg H2, compared to 13.2 kg for grey hydrogen. The industry’s best-case figure of 3.4 kg has never been consistently achieved at industrial scale.[1]

2

The Gulf has the cheapest solar energy on earth and is not using it. Saudi Arabia has achieved a world-record low LCOE of $10.40/MWh for solar electricity.[2] Blue hydrogen costs $65–70/MWh equivalent before any carbon pricing. The entire economic case for blue hydrogen as a “cost-competitive transition fuel” is incoherent in this context.

3

The consulting industry is growing on this advice. The GCC consulting market exceeded $6 billion in 2024, growing at four times the global average.[3] The fees for blue hydrogen and CCUS strategy work are estimated at $270M–$1.4B over 2020–2030. The firms bear no liability for the outcomes of their recommendations.

4

The climate damage is orders of magnitude larger than the fees. Applying the EPA’s central social cost of carbon ($190/tonne), the excess emissions from blue hydrogen relative to a green hydrogen alternative generate an estimated $38–76 billion in climate damage over the production horizon.[4] Every dollar of consulting fees generates an estimated $53–106 in climate damage.

5

The stranded asset risk falls entirely on Gulf governments. Blue hydrogen infrastructure has a designed lifespan of 25–30 years. Green hydrogen from Gulf solar is projected to reach cost parity with blue hydrogen by 2030–2031 under EU CBAM pricing, and by 2027 under $150/tonne carbon pricing. Assets committed today face near-certain stranding before end of life. The consulting firms will have been paid years earlier.

Section 01

The Science: Blue Hydrogen vs. Grey

The hydrogen colour taxonomy — green, blue, grey — is one of the most consequential acts of industrial marketing in the history of energy policy. It implies a spectrum from clean to dirty, with blue occupying a credible middle position. The peer-reviewed science tells a different story.

The methane problem

The gap between “ideal blue” (3.4 kg CO2eq) and “realistic blue” (12.0 kg CO2eq) is the single most important number in the entire blue hydrogen debate. It has two sources.

Fugitive methane. Methane leaks at every stage of the gas supply chain. At 1.5–3.5% leakage rates — the range observed across the gas industry — these leaks convert to enormous CO2-equivalent impacts because methane is 83 times more potent than CO2 over a 20-year horizon.[6] Research from Cornell and Stanford found that at observed leakage rates, the carbon footprint of blue hydrogen is more than 20% greater than burning natural gas directly for heat.[1]

CCS underperformance. The theoretical 95% capture rate assumed in industry projections has never been consistently achieved at industrial scale. The world’s largest blue hydrogen facility captures only approximately 70% of greenhouse gas emissions — far below the level needed to qualify as clean hydrogen under US or EU subsidy frameworks.[7]

“Blue hydrogen is hardly emissions free. Blue hydrogen as a strategy only works to the extent it is possible to store carbon dioxide long-term indefinitely into the future without leakage back to the atmosphere.”

Howarth & Jacobson, Energy Science & Engineering, 2021 — Cornell/Stanford[1]

Carbon capture: a technology that keeps failing

The Gulf’s entire blue hydrogen proposition rests on carbon capture working at scale. The historical record is not reassuring.

A statistical study of 263 CCUS projects between 1995 and 2018 found that most have failed, and that failure risk increases dramatically with project size — increasing capacity by 1 million tonnes of CO2 per year raises the risk of termination by nearly 50%.[8] The Gulf is planning projects at precisely the scale most likely to underdeliver.

Real-world precedents include: the Gorgon project in Australia, which missed CO2 capture targets entirely for its first 3.5 years; the Quest project in Canada, where carbon capture operations generated 1.16 Mt of CO2 in 2016–2020 — more than 20% of what was captured; and BP’s H2 Teesside blue hydrogen project in the UK, cancelled in December 2025 after years of development, citing land use conflicts, public opposition, and economic concerns.[9]

What carbon pricing does to the comparison

Blue hydrogen carries a residual carbon penalty from uncaptured CO2 and methane leakage. Solar carries none. As carbon pricing spreads — and EU CBAM has been live since January 2026 — this penalty compounds year on year.

At the EU CBAM level — already operative from January 2026 — blue hydrogen becomes more expensive than green hydrogen from Gulf solar within this decade. At the EPA’s own social cost of carbon ($190/t), that crossover has already happened. The “bridge fuel” has already been made redundant by the economics of the region it is supposed to serve.

Three-quarters of global fossil fuel stranded asset exposure belongs to governments, not private companies.[15] When blue hydrogen infrastructure becomes uncompetitive — as the cost trajectory indicates it will before end of designed life — the liability falls on sovereign treasuries. Methodology note

The damage-to-fee ratio is the most striking figure in this analysis and the most defensible when the methodology is shown. The defence rests on the EPA’s own central social cost of carbon and the Cornell/Stanford emissions data — both peer-reviewed or official government methodology. The range presentation is essential: this is a derived estimate, not an audited figure.

These estimates deliberately exclude: stranded asset losses from misallocated infrastructure; EU CBAM penalties on Gulf exports; the opportunity cost of capital not deployed in solar; and lock-in effects on gas infrastructure extending beyond 2040. Including these would substantially increase the damage figure.

McKinsey, BCG, and Bain & Company declined to provide fee data for Gulf energy transition engagements. All consulting fee figures in this report are estimates derived from publicly available market data using disclosed methodology. This report will be updated to reflect any response received.

Section 04

Stranded Assets: Who Pays When It Fails

A stranded asset is not a broken asset. It is an asset that loses economic value before the end of its intended working life because the world has moved on. Blue hydrogen infrastructure is particularly exposed because it carries three interlocked stranding risks that reinforce each other.

The three interlocked risks

1. Technology lock-in. Reformer plants, CCS systems, and CO2 injection networks are designed for 25–30 year operational lives and are irrevocably tied to natural gas feedstock. They cannot be repurposed for green hydrogen without capital-equivalent reinvestment.[17]

2. Cost trajectory. Green hydrogen from Gulf solar is projected to reach $1.50–2.00/kg in MENA by 2030 (IEA, IRENA).[11] Blue hydrogen currently costs $2.80–3.50/kg.[12] A blue hydrogen facility committed today will be out-competed before paying back its capital — potentially by 2030 under CBAM pricing.

3. Political lock-in. Because these investments are sovereign and publicly committed, they are unlikely to be shut down even when uncompetitive. The Gorgon CCS project in Australia continued operating through years of failure because political and reputational costs of closure exceeded financial losses. Gulf governments, having staked national strategy on these assets, face identical constraints.[18]

Section 06

The Counterfactual: What Good Advice Looks Like

The Gulf possesses three structural advantages for a solar-first energy transition that no other major economy on earth can replicate simultaneously.

Irradiance. Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Oman receive more than 2,000 kWh/m² annually — among the highest solar resources on the planet. This is a geological advantage. It does not depreciate and cannot be disrupted by geopolitics.

Land. A solar installation covering 1% of Saudi Arabia’s territory — roughly the size of Kuwait — would generate more electricity than Saudi Arabia currently consumes in a year.

Capital. The GCC sovereign wealth funds collectively hold more than $3.5 trillion in assets. The infrastructure capacity to deliver megaprojects at exceptional speed and scale already exists.

A Gulf sovereign wealth fund deploying $100B into utility-scale solar creates the world’s largest clean energy complex, producing electricity at $10–13/MWh for 30 years with no fuel cost, no carbon risk, no CCS failure risk, and no stranding risk. The same capital deployed in blue hydrogen infrastructure creates a 25–30 year liability to a gas feedstock, an unproven carbon capture technology, and a carbon pricing regime that makes the product more expensive every year.

Glossary

Key Terms

Blue hydrogen
Hydrogen produced from natural gas via steam methane reforming, with carbon capture and storage theoretically attached. Presented as “low-carbon” or “clean.” Under realistic operating conditions, it is nearly as carbon-intensive as grey hydrogen.
Grey hydrogen
Hydrogen produced from natural gas with no carbon capture. Currently the industrial standard, accounting for the majority of global hydrogen production.
Green hydrogen
Hydrogen produced by electrolysis of water using renewable electricity. Near-zero lifecycle emissions. Currently more expensive than grey or blue, but falling rapidly in cost — particularly in high-solar regions like the Gulf.
CCUS / CCS
Carbon Capture, Utilisation and Storage. Technology that captures CO2 before it enters the atmosphere. Historically underdelivers on capture rates. Most large-scale projects have failed to meet targets.
Fugitive methane
Methane that leaks during gas extraction, processing, or transport. At 20-year global warming potential (GWP-20), methane is 83 times more potent than CO2. The primary reason blue hydrogen’s real-world emissions are far higher than industry claims.
LCOE
Levelised Cost of Energy. The average cost per unit of energy generated over a plant’s lifetime, including construction, fuel, and operation. Gulf solar LCOE: $10.40/MWh (world record). Blue hydrogen equivalent: $65–70/MWh.
Social cost of carbon (SCC)
The estimated economic damage caused by emitting one additional tonne of CO2. US EPA central estimate: $190/tonne (2023). Used in this report to calculate the climate damage from blue hydrogen’s excess emissions.
CBAM
EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism. Live from January 2026. Charges carbon tariffs on imports of aluminium, steel, cement, fertiliser, electricity, and hydrogen. Gulf aluminium exports are immediately exposed. Chemicals follow by 2030.
Stranded asset
An asset that loses economic value before the end of its intended working life, typically because of policy shifts, technology change, or market disruption. Blue hydrogen infrastructure is at high risk of stranding before its 25–30 year designed lifespan ends.
Carbon lock-in
The situation where today’s investment in carbon-intensive infrastructure makes future decarbonisation progressively more expensive. Blue hydrogen infrastructure does not create a bridge to clean energy — it deepens dependence on gas systems.
GWP-20
Global Warming Potential over 20 years. Using GWP-20 rather than the more common GWP-100 significantly worsens blue hydrogen’s emissions profile, because methane’s warming impact is concentrated in the near term. The IPCC increasingly recommends GWP-20 for near-term climate assessment.
