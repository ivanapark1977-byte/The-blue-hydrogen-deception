## What this is

A sourced investigative research report examining the role of major management consulting firms in promoting blue hydrogen strategies to Gulf Cooperation Council governments, despite scientific evidence that blue hydrogen is nearly as carbon-intensive as grey hydrogen under realistic operating conditions.

The report covers:

- The lifecycle emissions gap between ideal and realistic blue hydrogen
- Why Gulf solar energy at $10.40/MWh makes blue hydrogen economically incoherent
- The history of CCUS project failures at scale
- An estimated financial asymmetry between consulting fees and climate damage
- The EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism and its live impact on Gulf exports
- The stranded asset risk facing governments who commit capital to blue hydrogen infrastructure
- What a responsible Gulf energy transition strategy would actually look like

---

## Key findings

1. **Blue hydrogen is nearly as damaging as grey.** Under realistic conditions — methane leakage of 1.5–3.5% and capture rates of 85–90% — blue hydrogen produces ~12 kg CO₂eq per kg H₂, versus 13.2 kg for grey hydrogen. The industry's best-case figure of 3.4 kg has never been consistently achieved at scale.

2. **The Gulf has the world's cheapest solar and is not using it.** Saudi Arabia has achieved a world-record low LCOE of $10.40/MWh. Blue hydrogen costs $65–70/MWh equivalent before any carbon pricing.

3. **Every dollar of consulting fees generates an estimated $53–106 in climate damage**, using the US EPA's central social cost of carbon ($190/tonne, 2023). All fee figures are estimates — actual figures are classified under Saudi law.

4. **The EU CBAM is already live.** From January 2026, Gulf aluminium and hydrogen exports face carbon tariffs at the EU border. Blue hydrogen's own carbon content makes it a liable product.

5. **Stranded asset risk falls on governments.** Three-quarters of global fossil fuel stranded asset exposure belongs to governments, not private companies (IPCC AR6).

## Methodology and sources

All estimates are presented as ranges and clearly labelled where they are derived rather than directly sourced. The full methodology for financial calculations is disclosed within the report.

McKinsey, BCG, and Bain & Company declined to provide fee data for Gulf energy transition engagements. All consulting fee figures are derived from publicly available market data.

Primary sources include:

- Howarth & Jacobson (2021), *Energy Science & Engineering* — blue hydrogen lifecycle emissions
- US EPA (2023) — Social Cost of Greenhouse Gases, $190/tonne central estimate
- Rystad Energy (2024) — Gulf solar LCOE
- Wood Mackenzie (2025) — LCOE trajectory forecasts
- Fuss et al. (2021) — CCUS project failure analysis, 263 projects 1995–2018
- Hydrogen Council (2024) — Gulf hydrogen investment data
- US Senate Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations (February 2024) — consulting firm testimony
- IPCC AR6 WGIII (2022) — stranded asset estimates

Full reference list with hyperlinks is included in the report.
